# English Grammar Rules, Syntax, and Structural Correctness

This reference guide contains rules for syntax, subject-verb agreement, common spelling conventions, and standard punctuation.

## 1. Subject-Verb Agreement
* Singularity: A singular subject requires a singular verb (e.g. "She writes clean code," NOT "She write clean code").
* Plurality: A plural subject requires a plural verb (e.g. "They write clean code").
* Compound Subjects: Joined by "and" usually take a plural verb. Joined by "or/nor" take a verb matching the closest subject.

## 2. Spelling Adjustments (American vs. British)
* "-or" vs "-our": American spelling uses "-or" (color, flavor, labor), while British spelling uses "-our" (colour, flavour, labour).
* "-ize" vs "-ise": American spelling uses "-ize" (organize, realize), while British spelling often uses "-ise" (organise, realise).
* "-er" vs "-re": American spelling uses "-er" (center, theater), while British spelling uses "-re" (centre, theatre).

## 3. Punctuation Conventions
* Commas: Used to separate independent clauses joined by coordinating conjunctions (and, but, for, or, nor, so, yet).
* Apostrophes: Used to indicate possession (e.g. "the developer's screen") or contractions ("it's" for "it is"). Do not confuse possessive "its" with contraction "it's".
* Quotation Marks: Place periods and commas inside quotation marks in American English; place them outside in British English.
